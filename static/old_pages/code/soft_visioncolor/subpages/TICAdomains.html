1. Orientation domains and the proposed analysis:


Back to top

SUMMARY: Plots at the left show experimental orientation domains in the surface of the V1 cortex of a cat [Ohki et al. Nature 2006] and in the surface of the V1 cortex of a ferret [measured at our lab by J. Brotons, 2016]. In both cases using intrinsic optical imaging (here you can see the experimental procedure, and some experimental data just for fun). Different colors represent different preferred orientation of the cells. Hyvärinen and Hoyer, proposed a functional explanation for this organization based on Topographic ICA: third plot from their paper [Vis. Res. 2001]. Here we propose a new analysis of the T-ICA result in the image space (as opposed to looking only at the T-ICA topology). The random scrambling of the oriented filters shown in our results (last plot) is incompatible with the smoothness found in the retina-cortex projection (see below). Therefore, contrarily to the original proposal, our results show that T-ICA does not explain the orientation domains in primates.


ORIENTATION DOMAINS MAY BE THOUGHT IN THE IMAGE SPACE: Left:Single cell measurements of receptive fields show that displacement across neurons in the cortical surface imply equivalent displacement of the corresponding receptive fields across the visual field. Note that the orientation of the receptive field veries smoothly according to the (colored) orientation preferences revealed by intrinsic imaging. Right: Stimulation with uniformly displaced vertical and horizontal lines at the retinal space gives rise to maximum stimulation at (slightly) distorted grid shown in the figure. This means that the retina-corex projection is smooth. This suggests that one may think on (slightly) distorted orientation domains projected back at the image space.



OUR PROPOSAL: Check the Topographic ICA results in the image space. The novel analysis consist of projecting the TICA topology back in the image space by representing the central location of the receptive field of each artificial neuron at the corresponding spatial position with the corresponding orientation-dependent color code. The experimental smoothness suggested by [Bosking et al. 02] implies that a proper theory would lead to locally continuous orientation domains also in the retinal space. Our results show that this is not the case for TICA.



2. Extra examples of continuity violations in the Topographic ICA literature:


Back to top

Orientation domains obtained from Topographic ICA have been reported by different groups: Hyvärinen et al. [Vis. Res. 2001, Springer 2009] and Ma et al. [Neurocomp. 2008]. Here we show that in every reported case it is easy to find violations of continuity of the orientation distribution in the image space.


Examples of continuity violation in [Hyvarinen and Hoyer, Vis. Res. 2001]: The figure shows that neurons which are far appart in the topology and hence have quite different tuning properties, are actually quite close in the retinal space. This represents a violation of the expected local continuity of the orientation domains in the retina. This is Fig. 2 of the work. See the detailed description and discussion there. Next figures show equivalent examples of violation of continuity in other papers.


Examples of continuity violation in [Hyvarinen, Hurri and Hoyer, Springer 2009]: Here the authors used a bigger pooling neighborhood in the TICA model, but it is easy to find examples of random mixture of sensors.


Examples of continuity violation in [Ma et al., Neurocomp. 2008]: Here the authors used an overcomplete version fo TICA, but, again, it is easy to find examples of random mixture of sensors.



3. Main Result: salt-and-pepper distribution of TICA oriented sensors


Back to top

DATA AND CODE: We built on the Hyvärinen and Hoyer Topographic ICA toolbox and associated database. However, in our work we explored a wider set of visual angles to check the consistency of the result over visual structures of different scale (visual angle in [0.64, 8.00] degrees). This implied building new training sets: we used the original visual angle and resolution (indicated in red), but also different sampling frequencies and discrete sizes to get different fields of view.

We ran Topographic ICA with default parameters over the new databases but we made a new analysis of the results: we fitted Gabors and looked at the distribution of the oriented filters in the retinal space to check for locally continuous orientation domains there as suggested by the results of Bosking et al. 2002.

The new databases and the new analysis require this specific Matlab CODE (see this README.txt for details). For your convenience, this specific code includes publicly available toolboxes ImageICA (by Hyvarinen and Hoyer) and BasicVideoTools (by Malo et al.). Please also acknowledge the authors of those toolboxes if you use our code.

The figure below shows the range of visual angles considered in the new training sets and the consistent salt-and-pepper discontinuous distributions obtained from the new analysis in the retina.



4. Full set of statistical tests on randomness


Back to top

TESTING THE EXISTENCE OF DISTINCT ORIENTATION DOMAINS: If the spatial distribution of sensors tuned to a certain orientation is more similar to a Cartesian grid than an equivalent sample from a 2D uniform distribution one can reject the hypothesis of presence of clusters (one can reject the existence distinct continuous orientation domains). In our tests the KL-divergence of the TICA distributions with a cartesian grid is compared to the empirical PDFs of the KL-div of uniform samples with a cartesian grid (see one example in the figure below). 5. Extra results for images of bigger complexity and other settings of the algorithm


Back to top


While discussing the violations of continuity of the orientation domains in the TICA literature [personal communication with Aapo Hyvarinen], he suggested exploring three issues: (1) trying different nonlinearities when enforcing sparsity in the learning, (2) trying different sizes in the complex-cell pooling (or different neighborhoods in the T-ICA topology), and (3) checking the results in images of higher complexity (larger PCA dimension, or bigger extension x bandwidth). Here we show that scrambled distributions of oriented filters are consistently obtained in every case. In most cases, complete convergence was achieved (as indicated by the Gabor-like nature of the functions shown below). However, in the higher complexity case convergence was not complete after several weeks of computation. Nevertheless, even in this yet-to-converge case it is clear that the oriented filters that already emerged are randomly scrambled in the image space. Therefore, the main message of the work is confirmed again.


RESULTS WITH DIFFERENT NONLINEARITIES: We modfied the estimate.m function in the TICA toolbox (see ExtendedImageICA HERE) to compare different nonlinearities to enforce the sparsity in the learning process.

Results with the default exponential (in green) and modified nonlinearities (in red, blue, black) are shown below: preferred location of sensors in the image space (see scrambled orientations), preferred frequencies, TICA functions and fitted Gabor functions.


RESULTS WITH DIFFERENT NEIGHBORHOODS: We modfied the estimate.m function in the TICA toolbox (see ExtendedImageICA HERE) to compare pooling regions of different size in the 2nd-stage mechanisms (complex cells).

Results with the default (3x3) and the modified pooling regions are shown below: preferred location of sensors in the image space (see scrambled orientations), preferred frequencies, TICA functions and fitted Gabor functions.


RESULTS WITH IMAGES OF DIFFERENT COMPLEXITY: Here complexity refers to the concept of Gabor information (bandwidth x spatial extension), or to the intrinsic dimensionality in terms of the PCA components that explain a big percentage of the energy of the signal. In the main result we already considered a dataset with bigger complexity than in the original works since we considered the case of 2 degrees (50x50 pixels) sampled at the same resolution as the case in the original works (images of size 32x32, or 1.28 degrees). In the rest of the cases, the bigger spatial extension was achieved at the cost of reducing the bandwidth (reducing the sampling frequency). Therefore, it can be argued that the complexity was not increased that much (even though according to the plot below, the 8 degree case has a PCA dimensionality which is 8 times bigger than the used in [Hyvärinen and Hoyer 01]). In order to explore a situation with even bigger complexity we considered the case of 4 degrees at the original sampling frequency (i.e. blocks of 100x100 pixels -factor 9 increase in PCA dimension-). In the following figure, highlighted in red, we can see: (1) the equivalence of the visual structures in the 100x100 case with the 50x50 case sampled at half the sampling frequency, and (2) the distribution of the Gabor-like functions in the image space (the scatter plot below the high resolution image).

This result is not complete because not all the functions converged to a Gabor-like function yet. CPU restrictions led us to kill the computation before it finished (after 3000 iterations of the algorithm running for one month in 20 CPUs). However, according to the results below, the observed behavior in the functions that did converge will not be modified by the remaining functions. Therefore, this partial observation confirms the main message: even when the complexity is substantially higher than in the original work (up to a factor 9 in the PCA dimension) the oriented filters that emerge are randomly scrambled in the image space.


The figures below compare the 100x100 result with the 50x50 result, and show the subset of functions analyzed. Results indicate that, certainly, there is still a long way to complete convergence: (a) there are many functions yet to converge (see the noisy functions not far from the white noise initialization, and see that the goal-function curve, in blue, is far from the plateau behavior shown in the other examples included in the paper), and (b) the distribution of orientations of the functions that converged is still not uniform (more vertical than horizontal functions). However, this seems like a computation time and not a fundamental issue. Note that complexity may be bigger in higher frequencies, but the visual structures at lower frequencies are the same as in the 50x50 case sampled at lower frequency. As a result, the uniform distribution of functions across orientations should appear after some extra iterations. In the same vein, it is fine to reason by looking at the location of the Gabor-like functions that already emerged. Emergence of new (higher frequency) functions later in the evolution of the algorithm is not going to change the behavior of the functions that already emerged before. And, in this partial 100x100 example, the functions that converged display random scrambling of orientation in the retinal space.
